HOME ABOUT ME CS50 MY SUMMER FINAL THOUGHTS

Starting my journey in Computer Science with CS50 turned out to be one of the best decisions of my life. Within just 15 minutes of the first lecture, I understood why this course has been recommended for years as the introductory course for tech enthusiasts who want to immerse themselves in the field.

Because this is what true inspiration looks like!

Amid thousands of courses out there that teach programming languages in a mechanical, rote manner, this gem delivers the true essence of problem-solving using computers. It doesn’t ask you to solve theoretical problems like checking for prime numbers in a long list of elements or solving complex equations faster than others (which, while not inherently bad, often stifles beginners’ creativity and limits the scope of learning to only a few gifted individuals). Instead, the course lets you dive into real-world applications — you see and practice how image filters work, how to manipulate audio files, and how to implement algorithms to check credit card validity or determine the reading grade of a book. You even learn how to recover data from a memory card, use SQL databases, and solve problems through creative games like Fiftyville.

You don't just solve practical problems — you gain a deeper understanding of how the world and technology function and evolve.

I also have no doubt that many people wish they had the privilege of learning from great teachers who possess even a fraction of the enthusiasm and professionalism that Professor David J. Malan exudes. It reminds me of when Bill Gates spoke of Richard Feynman as "the best teacher I never had."

I can’t help but imagine that someone taking part in this course today might one day revolutionize the field.

Looking back, this was truly a hilarious experience. There was a time when I thought there was no chance in a million years that I would attend the National Economics University. There is really no tell of the future as they say, I figured.

Not long after the graduation exam results were announced, I came down with a fever that lasted a whole week. In a haze and unable to sleep one night, I found myself wandering online and stumbled upon the Facebook page of the Faculty of Data Science and Artificial Intelligence at NEU. Out of mere curiosity, I scrolled through all the posts and checked out the interactions. I saw snippets of the Data Science and Artificial Intelligence discovery course and pictures from the signing of lab establishments. That’s when the idea of choosing NEU began to form in my mind. I joined the Facebook group and the Zalo chat group.

I came across Sir Hà Minh Hoàng's profile, where he shared information about a mathematical modeling contest, and I thought about participating. More than anything, I wanted an excuse to visit the Faculty before making my final decision. However, fear held me back; I felt I had no knowledge to compete. I was willing to join just for the experience, but the requirement to have a team made me hesitate. Surely, there were others who wanted to participate to win, and I didn’t want to hold them back.

When I finally made up my mind, the first round was already eight days in. The question involved a nurse scheduling problem, and with only two days left, I knew I couldn't tackle it alone. I gathered my courage and asked Mr. Hoàng for help. He agreed, and I was called to the lab, where a senior guided me through the problem. It was my first time working with CPLEX, which was quite interesting. The three weeks of Python learning I had just completed proved helpful, which made me feel proud of myself a little bit.

The funny part was that I didn't realize the problem statement spanned two pages (who would think that when the questions were posted online?). I ended up having to finish half of the task at home by myself. Surprisingly (or perhaps unsurprisingly, to be fair I did get outside help), I made it into the top 10 of the high school table and advanced to the second round.

During the second round, I was in Taiwan for the entire duration and frankly didn’t contribute much. All credit goes to my teammate (I owe him a lot). However, it was fascinating to be introduced to the problem of pandemic simulation and to work through it using MATLAB. While we didn’t make it into the top two and become finalists, I found it satisfying enough, knowing that many others were far more deserving (considering we had only known about the whole thing in less than 2 weeks).

Anyways, the meeting at the lab was the turning point that made me want to study at NEU. I figured that this is a dynamic environment, which I had long thought was unsuitable for my personality, but I promised myself to change — I knew I had to.

Honestly, I want to engage in the research field as soon as possible, but I’ll elaborate more on that in "Final Thoughts."

Those five days were filled with countless trials and errors. It was during this time that I truly realized how far we’ve come in terms of creative AI. I experimented with several AI tools, and I had noted them down, so here’s a quick list:

  • Creating Images: CGDream [https://cgdream.ai/]
  • Image/Text to Video: RunwayML [https://runwayml.com/], Tooncrafter [https://huggingface.co/spaces/Doubiiu/tooncrafter]
  • Watermark Removal: Media.io [https://anieraser.media.io/app], Vmake [https://vmake.ai/]
  • AI Filters for Images: Fotor [https://goart.fotor.com/]
  • Others: Photoshop, IbisPaint, Background Removal

For the competition, I opted for start frame - end frame video generation approach (which means the AI will do the sequences in between the 2 frames) rather than relying on prompt engineering (and let the AI dictates everything). I already had a specific set of scene cuts in mind, and I found prompt-based generation didn’t align with my vision. One issue was that the characters would often look Westernized, and I lacked control over certain scene-specific details. Plus, any dialogue would have to be in English, which wasn’t what I wanted. Paying for premium tools was an option, but I wasn’t keen on spending even $10 for the contest. So, congrats to the first-place winners! I think I stumbled across the tools they used, but their direction wasn’t quite what I was after. In the end, Tooncrafter and Photoshop were the two primary tools I worked with.

In hindsight, maybe a different approach could have been better:

  • Start with lyrics → Write music with Suno AI → Create images using Midjourney AI based on the music → Transform those images into a short video using RunwayML.

This approach would be costly, but it’s something I might experiment with next time—it seems like it could lead to something really interesting. I came across a video that followed a similar process (without the Midjourney part) after the contest had ended, and it’s stuck with me ever since: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IpP6XojxH3g.

This one is difficult to talk about. The course began with around 45 participants, but by the last day, only 7 remained. While there are many factors, I believe the main issues can be summarized as follows:

  1. Haste: As I mentioned earlier, NEU is a highly dynamic environment where everything moves quickly. However, this speed can sometimes come at the cost of thorough preparation and verification, leading to premature implementation.

  2. Lecturers: Assigning the teaching role to a student (even an academically excellent one) or someone with no prior teaching experience is rarely a good idea. Teaching, especially introductory material, requires a unique skill set. Many mistakenly believe it's easier to teach foundational concepts than advanced topics, but I’ve come to realize, especially after CS50, that setting a strong foundation requires exceptional skill.

  3. Program: The course lacked a clear outline from the beginning, and no one seemed to review the structure before it was delivered.

There are certainly more points to consider, but I think it's crucial that we reflect on this experience to prevent future repetitions.

So far the course has still at some basic stuffs, with Power BI, data selection techniques for reporting and visualizing, reading data and understanding different charts / graphs. I have got to learn about some new concepts like the CRISP-DM model, MECE rule, Pearson's Correlation Coefficient, RFM Analysis and probably many more to come. Once the couse ends, there will be more things for me to talk about.
